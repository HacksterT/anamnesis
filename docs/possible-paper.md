**The closest existing work is "Memory in the Age of AI Agents"** — a comprehensive survey from December 2025 (updated January 2026) that has gotten significant attention. It proposes a taxonomy distinguishing factual, experiential, and working memory, and analyzes how memory is formed, evolved, and retrieved over time. There's also an ICLR 2026 workshop (MemAgents) dedicated specifically to the memory layer for agentic systems, and a March 2026 position paper framing multi-agent memory as a computer architecture problem with shared vs. distributed paradigms.

**However — and this is where your opportunity is — there's a real gap.**

The existing literature is taxonomic and descriptive. The "Memory in the Age of AI Agents" survey distinguishes factual memory, experiential memory that captures procedural knowledge, and working memory that manages workspace information during task instances. Their taxonomy (factual/experiential/working) maps loosely onto your declarative/procedural/episodic, and they add a "forms" dimension (token-level, parametric, latent) that's about implementation mechanism rather than knowledge characteristics.

But here's what none of them do:

**Nobody provides a prescriptive decision framework.** The surveys describe *what exists* — they catalog implementations, propose taxonomies, compare frameworks. They don't answer "given a specific deployment scenario, how do I decide what memory architecture to use?" Your three-table model is a diagnostic tool, not a catalog. It says: assess your knowledge by kind, then by volume × dynamism, then layer on operational modifiers, and here's the specific engineering approach for each cell. That's a practitioner's framework, not a researcher's taxonomy.

**Nobody separates ingestion, retrieval, and injection as independent decision paths.** The existing work treats the memory lifecycle as a single pipeline (formation → evolution → retrieval). Your framework recognizes that the decision to embed at ingestion is independent of the decision to use vector search at retrieval, which is independent of the decision to inject pre-session vs. on-demand. These are three separate design choices with different drivers.

**Nobody uses volume and dynamism as the primary engineering selectors.** The existing taxonomies organize by memory *type* (what it is) or *form* (how it's implemented). Your Table 2 organizes by the two variables that actually determine the engineering approach. The insight that most niche agent deployments are small enough to skip retrieval entirely — and that the wiki pattern (compile at write time, inject wholesale at read time) outperforms RAG for bounded domains — isn't in the literature. The field has an implicit assumption that retrieval infrastructure is always needed.

**Nobody addresses multi-tenant deployment as a knowledge management problem.** The existing work focuses on single-agent or multi-agent architectures. Your framework treats the tenant as the knowledge boundary and the manifest as the deployment artifact. That's a platform engineering perspective that the research community isn't writing about because they're not building production multi-tenant systems.

**Nobody articulates the "compilation over retrieval" principle.** This is arguably your sharpest insight — that for bounded knowledge domains, investing in compilation quality (the wiki compiler, the nightly batch that distills transcripts into structured pages) produces better results than investing in retrieval sophistication (vector search, re-ranking, hybrid fusion). The field is heavily invested in retrieval innovation. Your framework argues that's often solving the wrong problem.

The angle is a **prescriptive decision framework for practitioners** building production agent systems. The three-table model, grounded in real deployment cases (personal assistant, SMB voice agent, multi-tenant platform), with the anti-patterns section calling out where the field's default assumptions lead to over-engineering. Position it against the "Memory in the Age of AI Agents" survey as: "they described the landscape; we provide the map for navigating it."

Your clinical informatics background is a genuine differentiator here. The triage metaphor isn't decorative — it's a methodology the field hasn't applied. Diagnostic decision frameworks are standard in clinical practice and virtually absent in agent architecture.
